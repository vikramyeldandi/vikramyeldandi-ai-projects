Retail Competitive
Intelligence Agent

An AI analyst that researches any retailer on demand — running 6 targeted web searches, synthesising live results, and producing a structured competitive brief with executive summary, financials, SWOT, strategic signals, watch list, and cited sources.

01 — Problem & Objective

Automating the work of a retail analyst

In a buying, strategy, or merchandising team, competitive intelligence is gathered manually — a researcher spends hours searching news, earnings releases, trade press, and analyst reports before synthesising findings into a brief. That brief reaches stakeholders days later, often stale by the time it's read.

The objective was to build an agent that compresses that research cycle from hours to minutes — running multiple targeted searches, grounding every claim in a source, and producing a consistently structured brief that a strategy team could act on immediately.

The Problem

Manual CI research is slow, inconsistent in structure, and often outdated by the time it reaches decision-makers. Teams rely on whoever has time to search, not whoever knows what to look for.

The Objective

An on-demand CI agent that runs 6 targeted searches, synthesises live results into a structured brief, and cites every claim — producing analyst-grade output in under 60 seconds.

02 — How It Works

Multi-query retrieval and synthesis pipeline

The key design decision was not to rely on a single search. A single query like "Zara news" returns only the most recent headlines. Real CI requires targeted retrieval across different dimensions — financial performance, strategy, product moves, challenges, and leadership signals — each needing a different query.

1
User inputs retailer name

Any retailer — global or regional, public or private, fashion or home or grocery. No constraints on market or size.

2
6 targeted web searches run in sequence

Recent news → financial results → strategy & expansion → product & innovation → challenges & headwinds → leadership & brand. Each query targets a different intelligence dimension.

3
Claude synthesises across all results

All search results are passed to the model with a strict system prompt. The model synthesises, reconciles conflicting sources, and structures the output. No claim is allowed without a source.

4
Structured brief rendered in the UI

The brief renders as a formatted document with 7 sections. Sources are cited inline. The brief can be copied as markdown for sharing in Slack, Notion, or email.

03 — Brief Structure

7 sections — the same template every time

Consistency of structure is as important as quality of content. A brief that looks different every time creates cognitive load for the reader. The system prompt enforces the exact same 7-section template regardless of which retailer is searched.

🔍
Executive Summary

2-3 sentences on current strategic position and the single most important thing happening to this business right now.

📰
Recent Developments

4-6 significant news items from the last 90 days, each with date, event, and why it matters — with source URLs.

💰
Financials & Performance

Revenue, trend, key markets, profitability. Figures always include reporting period. Private companies flagged as "not publicly disclosed."

⚡
SWOT Analysis

3-4 specific, evidence-based points per quadrant. Not generic retail observations — grounded in what this retailer is actually doing.

📡
Strategic Signals

3-5 signals showing what the retailer is visibly prioritising, each with a sourced implication for competitors or the market.

👁️
Watch List

2-3 forward-looking items that could materially change this retailer's position in the next 6-12 months.

04 — System Prompt Design

Enforcing analyst-grade output quality

The system prompt does the work that a research brief template would do in a real team. It defines the search strategy, the output schema, and a set of quality rules that prevent the model from fabricating, speculating, or producing vague output.

Quality Rules (excerpt) 
// Grounding rules — no fabrication
Every claim must be grounded in a search result.
Never fabricate data. If a figure is not verifiable,
say "not publicly disclosed."

// Citation rules
Financial figures: always include the reporting period
(e.g. "FY2024", "Q3 2025").
If conflicting information exists across sources,
note the discrepancy.

// Specificity rules
SWOT must be specific to this retailer —
not generic retail observations.
Strategic Signals must be backed by at least
one recent source each.
Watch List must be forward-looking,
not restatements of current news.
05 — Concepts Demonstrated

What this agent teaches

🌐
Tool Use — Web Search

Claude's native web search tool runs live queries against the web at generation time. Results are current, not cached — the brief reflects what's happening today, not what was in training data.

🔍
Multi-Query Retrieval

6 targeted searches across different intelligence dimensions — not a single broad query. Each search targets a specific failure mode in CI: missing financials, missing strategy signals, missing challenges.

📋
Structured Output Design

The system prompt enforces a consistent 7-section template. Same structure every time, regardless of retailer. This is a product design decision — consistency reduces cognitive load for the reader.

🔗
Source Grounding

Every claim is cited. The model is instructed to attribute findings to specific sources and flag data gaps when information is not publicly available — no hallucinated facts.

📊
SWOT as a Product Artefact

A SWOT is only valuable if it's specific. The system prompt explicitly prohibits generic retail observations — every point must be grounded in something this retailer is actually doing or facing.

📤
Exportable Output

The brief is copyable as markdown — ready to paste into Slack, Notion, Confluence, or email. A real deliverable, not just a demo output.

06 — Lessons Learned

What I would do differently

1

Multi-query retrieval is non-negotiable for quality CI. A single search returns the most recent headline — not the full picture. The 6-query strategy was the single most important decision in this agent. In production, query design is itself a product discipline — the queries should be reviewed and refined based on which ones consistently return weak results.

2

Private company coverage is a real limitation. For publicly listed retailers, the brief is strong — earnings, analyst commentary, and press releases provide rich source material. For private companies (Primark, Zara's parent Inditex is public but many regional players are not), the brief has visible data gaps. In production, this would be supplemented with paid data sources like Euromonitor, Mintel, or Companies House filings.

3

The Watch List is the most valuable section — and the hardest to get right. Executive summary and recent news are largely a retrieval task. The Watch List requires genuine forward-looking synthesis — the model must reason about what is likely to matter in 6-12 months, not just what is currently happening. This is where prompt engineering has the most leverage.

4

Source quality varies significantly. Web search returns a mix of authoritative sources (Reuters, FT, Bloomberg) and low-quality aggregators. In production, the system prompt should include domain allowlists — prioritise Financial Times, Reuters, Bloomberg, company investor relations pages, and major trade publications over SEO-optimised content farms.

5

This agent should trigger weekly, not on-demand. The most valuable version of this agent runs automatically every Monday for a watchlist of 10-20 tracked competitors and delivers briefs to a Slack channel or email digest. On-demand is useful for ad hoc research. Scheduled delivery is what makes CI a habit rather than a project.
